Phase 1 — Learn the Language of Games (0–3 months)

You do not start by making your dream game.

You start by learning:

- why games feel good,
- how players think,
- how game loops work,
- how to prototype quickly.

1. Learn Game Design Fundamentals

Start here:

Must-read books

- The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses
- A Theory of Fun for Game Design
- Level Up! The Guide to Great Video Game Design
- Blood, Sweat, and Pixels

- player motivation,
- loops,

2. Learn to Analyze Games

Do not just play games.

Reverse-engineer them.

For every game ask:

- What is the core loop?
- Why is movement satisfying?
- Why does exploration feel rewarding?
- How is tension created?
- How does UI disappear into immersion?
- What emotions does the game target?

Create a “game analysis notebook.”

This becomes your future studio DNA.

The Core Framework

You need 3 layers of notes:

1. Observation Notes
2. Design Deconstruction Notes
3. Studio Philosophy Notes

Most people only do #1.

The real value comes from #2 and #3.

  

Your Main Framework

O → D → P → A

This is the framework I’d strongly recommend.

O — Observe

What happened?

Pure facts.  
No interpretation yet.

Example:

- Player walks slowly during storm.
- Camera shakes slightly.
- Music disappears.
- Visibility reduced.
- Footsteps become louder.

Objective observations only.

  

D — Deconstruct

Why did it work?

Break apart:

- mechanics,
- timing,
- audio,
- psychology,
- visuals,
- pacing.

Example:  
“The storm slows movement which increases vulnerability. Reduced visibility increases uncertainty. Silence amplifies environmental sounds.”

Now you're studying systems.

  

P — Principle

Extract universal design truth.

This is the GOLD.

Example:

“Reducing information increases emotional tension.”

OR:

“Traversal difficulty creates emotional investment.”

This becomes reusable across ALL future games.

  

A — Application

How can YOU use this?

Now convert insight into experiments.

Example:

- Fog mechanic
- Flashlight battery system
- Audio-only enemy cues
- Dynamic weather traversal

This transforms passive learning into studio DNA.

  

Example Full Breakdown

Let’s analyze:  
Death Stranding

  

O — Observe

- Cargo shifts while walking.
- Terrain angle affects balance.
- Player constantly adjusts movement.
- Long silent traversal sections.
- Weather damages cargo.
- Small obstacles matter.

  

D — Deconstruct

- Traversal itself is gameplay.
- Micro-instability keeps engagement high.
- Environmental friction creates emotional tension.
- Cargo creates psychological responsibility.
- Silence amplifies isolation.

  

P — Principle

“Physical friction can create emotional attachment.”

This is a VERY high-level design insight.

  

A — Application

Potential future mechanics:

- fragile light source,
- unstable climbing gear,
- injured companion affecting movement,
- vehicle degradation systems,
- environmental resistance traversal.

Now you've transformed:  
“cool mechanic”  
into:  
reusable design philosophy.

That’s elite-level thinking.

PART 4 — How To ACTUALLY Take Notes While Playing

This is the key.

Most people write too much useless detail.

You need:

observation compression.

  

While Playing:

ONLY capture:

- emotional reactions,
- design patterns,
- memorable moments,
- pacing shifts,
- psychological tricks,
- mechanics interacting emotionally.

NOT story summaries.

  

Example BAD Note

“There was a forest area with enemies.”

Worthless.

  

GOOD Note

“Dense vegetation reduced visibility, making audio cues more important than visuals.”

THAT becomes reusable.
